# Fast-Food Chains 2021 — Descriptive Analysis & Insights

*Presenter narrative accompanying the Power BI report. The central question runs through every page: in fast food, do you win by scaling **fast** or scaling **right**? My argument is the latter — the **segment** a chain competes in and its **per-store economics** decide success, while raw store count is a by-product, not a cause. Each page below is read in three parts — what the visual shows, the numbers behind it, and my interpretation — closing with the relevance for a B2B food supplier such as Kamereo.*

---

## Page 0 — Cover: framing the question

### My read

The analysis examines the world’s 50 largest fast-food chains using a 2021 snapshot. Together, these chains generated approximately $248 billion in systemwide sales. Sales productivity varies substantially across brands, with a 21-fold difference in sales per unit, indicating considerable heterogeneity in store performance. The study focuses on revenue and store-level productivity rather than profitability and uses analyst-assigned market segments to explore how scale and operational efficiency relate to growth.

### Relevance for Kamereo
Kamereo sells *into* restaurants; its customers are chains like these. If store count does not predict store strength, the ideal customer is not the chain with the most outlets but the one whose individual stores are strong and sit in a resilient segment — because for a supplier, a single high-volume store is a larger, more durable account than several weak ones. The cover sets up that principle: **bet on the unit, not the logo.**

## Page 1 — The Landscape: does size mean strength?

### My read
Sales are concentrated among a handful of brands—McDonald’s alone contributes the largest share of U.S. systemwide sales, and the cumulative curve indicates that relatively few chains account for most industry revenue. However, having the most locations does not imply superior store economics: the two rankings chart show something interesting: by put the biggest chains and the strongest-per-store chains side by side, they barely overlap.**Footprint and store-level productivity are independent variables** — being large is not the same as being good, and Subway is the proof, leading on stores while trailing on every store's output. The chains that top the per-store list (Chick-fil-A, Raising Cane's, In-N-Out) win with far fewer, far stronger locations. This page is the empirical core of the whole report: *scale right, not fast.* Size is what you end up with, not what makes you win.

### Relevance for Kamereo
For a supplier, an account's value is set by the volume of each store, not the number on the sign. Targeting by footprint alone would over-index on low-productivity giants — a 21,000-store Subway account is a lot of weak stores, while one Chick-fil-A location is worth roughly fourteen of them per store. The landscape says Kamereo should qualify restaurant customers on **per-store strength**, using scale only as a secondary multiplier.

## Page 2 — Drivers: what makes a store strong?

### My read
If Page 1 showed that size and strength come apart, this page works through why. Start with the ceiling: group the chains by food type and chicken stores sell the most per unit (~$2.6K), burgers sit just behind (~$2.3K), and pizza trails at the bottom (~$1.0K). The segment you compete in sets a rough ceiling — a category advantage worth about 2.6× from top to bottom.

But the ceiling isn't the outcome, and the next two charts rule out the obvious levers. Plot sales per store against store count and the cloud is flat — adding locations doesn't lift per-store sales. The biggest chains sell an average amount per store, while the best per-store sellers have far fewer stores. Franchising tells the same non-story: the points scatter at every level, so franchise mix doesn't predict per-store sales — Chipotle earns ~$2.6K per store at 0% franchise, yet the highest per-store performers sit at the fully-franchised end, and the biggest franchisors cluster around the average. Neither scale nor franchise mix is the thing that makes a store strong.

What separates stores is execution *inside* the category. The best and worst chain in a single segment are further apart than the segments are from each other — chicken alone runs from roughly $1K to $6K per unit, a wider gap than the entire chicken-to-pizza spread. So category sets the ceiling; execution decides where under it you land. **Perfect the unit, then earn the scale.**

### Relevance for Kamereo
For a supplier, this is the freeing part: you don't have to guess which logo wins. Segment is a cheap first screen — some categories simply sell more per store — but the real signal is the account's own execution, which shows up directly in order size and reorder rate, not in its logo or franchise count. A well-run small customer that executes can open 10–20 stores a year and compound into a major account — exactly the long-tail bet Kamereo already makes. Qualify on the **operator**, not the format.
