Everything else about categories comes from company materials, not the video.

**Part B — Extra research (outside the video, verified online sources only):** Kamereo positions itself as a one-stop platform, and its own site describes a catalog of roughly 1,000–3,000+ products spanning **fresh vegetables and fruits, meat, seafood, dried/dry goods, beverages, alcohol, and pantry items like sauces and honey** — i.e., fresh produce is the founding core, but the range deliberately covers most of what a restaurant kitchen orders daily so buyers don't have to split orders across suppliers. ## Q4: What key performance metrics does Kamereo focus on to drive growth?

### Answer
Từ video, tôi thấy được taku strategy là khiến kamereo trở thành one-stop platform, vậy performance metrics kamereo đang đặt ra là:
Về customer service: on-time delivery rate, error-free products, order-fill(fulfillment) rate.
Về B2b growth: new sales and net revenue retention for account breadth, order count and average order value for account depth.

### 🤖 AI-generated — supplementary research

**Part A — From the video:** The episode doesn't lay out a metrics dashboard, but it points clearly at *which* metrics Taku thinks matter. His whole downturn argument is to focus on what's controllable — **operational efficiency and supply-chain optimization** — rather than macro/market conditions. In practice that means the numbers he cares about are the operational ones (delivery reliability, quality consistency, cost discipline), not vanity growth figures. His advice to new entrants — start small, don't over-scale, prioritize consistent quality over marketing spend — is the same idea: watch unit-level operational health before top-line size.

**Part B — Extra research (outside the video, verified online sources only):** Kamereo's own framing makes **sales the ultimate KPI**, but deliberately decomposed rather than tracked as a single number — into **new sales, existing/retention sales, order count, and order size (average order value)**, measured per rep, per team, and company-wide, and benchmarked against targets, historical performance, and competitors. That decomposition is the growth engine: more accounts × higher retention × more frequent orders × bigger baskets. Sitting underneath sales are the **operational service metrics** Kamereo publishes: **99%+ on-time delivery, 98%+ full-order fulfillment, and 99.5%+ error-free products**, with an order-cutoff-to-6AM next-morning delivery promise — these are the reliability numbers that drive the retention half of the sales equation. Finally, leadership describes a **"virtuous growth cycle"** (SMBC's words): acquiring more customers improves procurement terms, which improves customer economics, which wins more customers — so scale/volume metrics feed directly back into margin. Kaizen (continuous operational improvement) is named as the cultural discipline holding all of this together.

So the hierarchy: sales as the headline KPI, broken into acquisition + retention + order count + order size; operational reliability metrics (delivery, fulfillment, accuracy) as the leading indicators that protect retention; and procurement/scale economics as the flywheel that compounds it.

## Q6: What are Kamereo's new product categories, and what are the reasons behind their development?

### Answer
Các sản phẩm mới từ Kamereo là fresh seafood (tôm sống), ready-to-eat products(pre-washed salads) dành cho busy, live alone customers.
Lý do về việc phát triển các sản phẩm này là vì các nhà hàng ngày càng ưu tiên sản phẩm giá tốt hơn là thương hiệu. Vì vậy Kamereo tận dụng một thực tế ở Việt Nam — có rất nhiều nhà máy OEM (vốn sản xuất để xuất khẩu) đang không chạy hết công suất. Bằng cách đặt hàng sản xuất nhãn riêng qua phần công suất dư này, Kamereo có được sản phẩm giá rẻ hơn nhưng chất lượng tương đương hoặc tốt hơn, từ đó tăng khả năng cạnh tranh về giá.

### 🤖 AI-generated — supplementary research

**Part A — From the video:** The episode names two of the newer lines directly. The **private-label range is expanding into fresh seafood** (22:18–23:44), on top of the existing consumables, and Kamereo is **launching a "ready-to-eat" line** — pre-washed salads and similar convenience products aimed at busy consumers (23:57–24:49). The reasoning Taku gives fits the episode's whole theme: in a tough market you win on the controllables — cost and quality — so moving into private label and finished/near-finished goods is about owning more of the value chain (better margins, tighter quality control) rather than chasing market tailwinds.

**Part B — Extra research (outside the video, verified online sources only):** Beyond the core raw-ingredient catalog, Kamereo has been building out four newer lines, each with a clear strategic reason.

The **marketplace model** lets third-party producers store inventory in Kamereo's warehouses while Kamereo handles sales, delivery, and payment collection — essentially Amazon-FBA for HORECA food supply. The first partner was GYOMU JAPAN / Gyomu Super (Kobe Bussan), adding ~450 products. The reason: it lets Kamereo monetize its existing warehouse, last-mile network, and customer traffic *without* tying up its own capital in inventory, while giving small suppliers a route to HORECA buyers they couldn't reach alone.

The **private label** has three threads: pre-cut vegetables and fruit for supermarkets and convenience stores; private-labeled consumables made using the spare capacity of Vietnamese OEM factories that normally produce for export; and fresh seafood. The reasons: higher margin than reselling other brands, better price competitiveness (OEM excess capacity means lower cost at comparable or better quality), and riding Vietnam's shift from wet markets to modern retail with its rising demand for food safety and traceability.

The **ready-to-eat line** (pre-washed salads and similar) extends private label into finished goods, serving busy consumers and capturing more value per item.

And **direct imports** are cited as a growth area alongside the marketplace, broadening the catalog and giving Kamereo more control over sourcing.

The common thread behind all four: they're **margin- and control-oriented moves** that leverage assets Kamereo already owns (warehouses, cold-chain logistics, customer relationships, supplier network). Rather than just reselling other people's products at thin distribution margins, Kamereo is climbing the value chain — owning the brand, the processing, and the shelf — which is exactly the cost-and-quality discipline the video frames as how you survive a hard market. Geographic expansion (Hanoi from Dec 2024, central Vietnam planned) runs in parallel as the distribution side of the same growth push.
