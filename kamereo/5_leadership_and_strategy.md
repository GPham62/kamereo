# Kamereo — Leadership, Culture & Strategy
*Drawn from the Vietnam Innovators podcast episode ("Điều gì làm nên thành công của KAMEREO?", Jul 2024 — see `6_video_notes_cwTA57OEEnQ.md` for the timestamped episode summary and sourcing notes), a closely related fireside-chat writeup from Genesia Ventures (Kamereo's seed/Series-A investor), and the FedEx "Confessions of a CEO" interview.*

## From the video (see 6_video_notes_cwTA57OEEnQ.md for full detail)
Episode throughline: resilience in F&B during a tough economic stretch. Highlights: keep investor relationships warm between raises; during downturns focus on controllables (ops efficiency, supply chain) over macro conditions; advise new entrants to start small and not over-scale; the Đà Lạt→HCMC just-in-time produce logistics; private label expanding into fresh seafood; new "ready-to-eat" product line; ongoing Vietnam F&B Conference collaboration.

## Taku Tanaka — background
- Japanese, born 1989. Studied at University of Washington, graduated Keio University.
- 2012–2014: Credit Suisse Securities Japan, equity sales for institutional investors — saw firsthand how saturated/mature Japan's F&B market was (giants like Toyota/Honda-scale incumbents already own every lane).
- 2014: left banking, traveled Southeast Asia.
- 2015: joined **Pizza 4P's** as COO (Vietnam), overseeing finance, HR, procurement, Hanoi operations — grew it from 1 store to 10 in 3 years (2015-2018) after intentionally staying at 1 store for ~4 years first to perfect the operating model and brand.
- Jun 2018: founded **Kamereo** with co-founder Hiroshi Tokaku.

## Why Vietnam, why this problem
- In Japan, "it is quite difficult to find problems that are large enough to solve" — systems are already well-organized by large corporations/government.
- Vietnam offered "simple but significant problems... not yet solved," specifically in food supply: unstable pricing, unpredictable supply/demand, limited traceability, transactions without proper invoices, late deliveries, poor customer support.
- Explicit personal belief: **the best opportunities are the ones that look "too complicated or too hard to solve"** — easy-to-execute opportunities attract easy competition; hard structural problems (supply chain, logistics infrastructure) create durable moats because few competitors have the patience/capability to attempt them.

## Sales & go-to-market philosophy
- **Never lead with price.** In a price-sensitive market like Vietnam, competing on price alone is "a losing game" — "if price alone decides the outcome, then we wouldn't even need salespeople." Reps are trained to first surface the customer's actual pain points (multi-supplier hassle, missing legal invoices, inconsistent quality) and sell the combined value, not the price sheet.
- **Long-tail SME strategy, not enterprise-only.** Vietnam lacks Japan/Korea-style national giants (Toyota, Honda, Samsung, LG) — even Vingroup is comparatively small — so reaching billion-dollar scale requires a large sales force penetrating thousands of SMEs individually, not a handful of whale accounts. Upside: a small customer that succeeds might open 10-20 new stores a year, compounding account revenue fast — a benefit unique to fast-growing, still-small B2B markets where big incumbents haven't yet found it worthwhile to enter.
- **Timing is everything** — move too late and it becomes very hard to compete once bigger players do enter.

## Organizational / culture philosophy
- **Design culture from Day One**, not later — in Vietnam, headcount can jump from ~100 to 400-500 employees right after a Series C round (much faster than Japan/Korea, where expensive/scarce labor keeps even Series B/C companies under 100 people). This compressed hyper-growth means cultural drift compounds quickly if values aren't defined early.
- **No compromise on hiring for values fit**, even under staffing pressure. Taku's own words: "if you're unsure, it's better not to hire." His single biggest self-identified failure across the company's history is hiring people who didn't share Kamereo's values/mission — creating organizational drag that was costly to unwind later. When a mismatch becomes clear, the founder must act to let that person go quickly, even though it's difficult.
- **Incentive design:** base salary + performance bonus (structured monthly/quarterly/annually depending on team), but deliberately not money-only — younger Vietnamese talent especially is described as motivated by "connection, fun, and growth opportunities," so Kamereo invests in learning, networking, and growth alongside financial incentives.
- **KPI philosophy:** the ultimate KPI is sales, decomposed into new sales, existing/retention sales, order count, and order size — tracked per person/team/company and benchmarked against targets, historical performance, and competitors.
- Training/mindset: company admits it under-invested in formal training historically, and is now shifting to hire younger talent and invest more deliberately in mindset/skills development — but only where the person is already open to new ideas ("if someone refuses to embrace new ideas, you can't really develop them").

## Investor characterization of the culture
SMBC Venture Capital (Series B investor) specifically credits Kamereo's culture around **"Kaizen"** (continuous improvement, a Japanese management concept) as core — "steadily advanced through persistent sales activities and operational improvements... creating a virtuous growth cycle where acquiring more customers improves procurement conditions, making it easier to attract even more customers."

## Reflections on what he'd change
- Asked what he'd do differently starting over: **same opportunity** (food supply chain), but "run the business smarter" — specifically, focus earlier on hiring the right people and designing a strong, scalable organization from the outset, rather than reactively bolting on culture/process later.

## Relevance to interview framing
This leadership material is useful context if the interview test expects strategic or business-judgment commentary alongside data analysis — Kamereo's own leadership explicitly frames success around (1) not competing on price, (2) long-tail account penetration, (3) operational/supply-chain complexity as a moat, and (4) culture/hiring discipline as the actual scaling bottleneck, not capital or technology per se.
